Brazilian folklore

CURUPIRA

"The curupira, one of the most famous characters in Brazilian folklore , is known as a mythical being that protects the forest. Its legend has its origins in indigenous peoples, being very famous in the North of Brazil , especially in Amazonas and Pará. This legend is quite old, with mention of it from the 16th century."

VITÓRIA RÉGIA

Vitória Régia is a sweet character of Brazilian Folklore. She walks along the rivers of the forest under a large round leaf, handing out flowers to all the animals and their friends. His legend is one of the best known in our Brazilian folklore, and it originated in the cultural traditions of the north of the country, due to being born in this region of the country. For the Indians, the moon was Jaci, with whom the little Indian girl Naiá was in love, until she drowned after trying to kiss the reflection of the moon. After drowning, she was saved by other plants, which took her in, transforming her into the Vitória Régia.

BOITATÁ

Boitatá is the friendliest snake in our folklore. Animals never know where she hides. She lives crawling through the forest with a flame of fire that envelops her entire body, and illuminates everything she passes. It is the light snake of Brazilian folklore. The legend of Boitatá is of indigenous origin, and the word Boitatá, in the Tupi-Guarani language, means snake (ox) of fire (tata). And whenever someone threatens to destroy the forest, she appears to frighten and protect the forest.

IARA

Iara is a very vain mermaid. She loves to sing and her singing hypnotizes the wicked. She also brings plants and algae from the bottom of the river to cure animals of diseases. She is the sweet enchanting mermaid of Brazilian folklore. Iara's story tells that she owns an enviable beauty. Despite coming from the Amazon region, the Legend of Iara is known throughout Brazil. Depending on the Brazilian region, the representation of India may differ, for example, in the color of her eyes and hair, which are either dark or light.

Headless Mule

The Headless Mule is an illustrious character from our folklore. With fire instead of her head, she lives there on the farm, along with horses, chickens and cows, next to the little church, and every day she goes out to the forest and plays with the other animals. She is always on the lookout, helping to avoid bushfires. She is the nice little mule of Brazilian folklore. In Brazil, the legend of the Headless Mule spread in rural areas, in the sugarcane area of ​​the Northeast and in the interior of the Southeast of the country. Its origin is closely linked to the Catholic Church. The little mule from the Turma do Folclore always lives close to the church and goes out dancing at night.
